sawdustcharlie 's review

Go to review page

3.0

I'm torn on this book. On the one hand, its a fabulous read. The characters are so well developed, and respond well to the situations Card has unfold in the book. There are characters I really come to care about, and even the "villains" are flushed out and multi-dimensional. Its well-written and engrossing.

Its a very dated book, though. I first read this book 10 years ago, as a teenager. Going back and reading it as an adult made me see lots of things I missed when I was younger.

First off, Card wrote the tensions on earth as chiefly between a western block, lead by the U.S., and an eastern block, lead by Russia. The book was first written in 1977, when the Cold War was still, well, cold. It reads as very dated now.

Secondly, the book reads as a paranoid dystopia. Children are our warriors, and violent psychopaths are able to come to power over formerly democratic/republican countries. Religion is taboo, practiced only with great shame and in secret by characters in the book. Is this the future Card envisions for us if we have a centralized government (that unites to fight the 'buggers'?).

Lastly, it seems that patriarchy remains well ensconced in Card's future. There are few girls that make it to Battle School in the book. Card writes that centuries of evolution are working against them in excelling how they need to so as to achieve the high standards demanded in battle school. As an adult female, that passage read like nails on a chalkboard to me . The only female student in the book, Petra, is the first to breakdown under the strain Ender places on her. No exactly the messages I would want a daughter of mine to read as a youngster.

On the face of it, this is a good book. I want to love it. Reading it with a critical eye, though, makes me see the undercurrents I missed as a teen--and even the blatant sexism. I am left, then, with an insanely readable book that I just can't classify as good.
